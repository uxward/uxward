# uxward.com — Design Brief

**Prepared for:** Whiteport Design — analyst and designer team
**Subject:** Personal portfolio site for Brandon E.B. Ward
**Domain:** uxward.com
**Status:** Strategic and structural brief finalized. Visual design and build pending.
**Date:** 2026

---

## 1. Project at a glance

uxward.com is a **recruitment-grade personal portfolio** for Brandon E.B. Ward, a 25-year design leader currently serving as CXO at Precocity and founder of Speakeazy (an AI-native SaaS for voice actors). The site exists to position him for senior design leadership roles at flagship technology companies.

The name *uxward* is a wordplay on his surname (Ward) combined with a directional verb — it operates as both URL and quiet brand wordmark. His name remains the primary identity; uxward is the home.

**One-line site description:** *A working journal of a founder/builder of design practice — selected work, occasional essays, and the things he's currently pointed at — published when there's something worth saying.*

---

## 2. Strategic position

### The recruitable claim

Brandon has been building design practice from scratch for 25 years — at four startups, two agencies, two senior consulting roles, and at Precocity for the last decade. He runs across modes (strategy, creative direction, hands-on production) with a fluency most senior leaders have lost. The AI-era expression of the same instinct is shipping his own tools — Speakeazy, FORGE, Verity, RAD, and a Figma plugin.

The positioning sentence the site is built to communicate:

> *"I build design practice from scratch and run it across every mode. Unlike most leaders at my level, I'm still shipping with my own hands, in the new tools, every week."*

This positioning was selected deliberately over generic alternatives like "senior strategist with receipts" because it is **more specific and more recruitable**. Every flagship company has fifty senior strategists in their pipeline. Builders who have stood up practice seven times and are also actively shipping AI-era tooling are rare.

### Audience

**Primary:** Senior design hiring managers and talent partners at flagship technology and design companies — LEGO, Netflix, SpaceX, Apple, Airbnb, and similar.

**Secondary:** Design community peers, potential consulting clients, future collaborators. These are downstream beneficiaries; the design is optimized for the primary audience.

### Success criteria — the 30-second test

A senior hiring manager scanning the site for 30 seconds must register four things, in this order:

1. Does this person think clearly?
2. Have they done real work?
3. Do they have a specific point of view?
4. Would I want them in a room with my team?

If the homepage answers all four within 30 seconds, the click through to the Precocity case study (the load-bearing artifact) comes naturally. If it answers none, no amount of visual polish will save it.

## 3. Voice and tone

**Voice rule, locked:** *Dry, not zany. The senior person at the dinner party who's clearly the most experienced one there but isn't trying to prove it.*

Brandon's actual personality is more playful than the site presents. The site is deliberately the **professional first-impression version** — confident, plainspoken, occasionally pointed. The wit is in the precision of the language and the willingness to name something plainly that other people won't. Not in jokes, exclamation points, emoji, or "hey there!" greetings.

### Voice rules

- **First-person throughout** ("I built…" "I think…" "I'm working on…"). Third-person reserved for the formal bio only.
- **No hedge words** — "kind of," "sort of," "I think maybe," "we might say" are banned. Senior people make claims; they don't hedge them.
- **No jargon or marketing language** — no "leveraged," "best-in-class," "synergies," "thought leader." If a 25-year design leader wouldn't say it out loud, don't write it.
- **Specific over generic** — "eight designers in eighteen months" beats "scaled the team." Real numbers, real teams, real names.
- **Trusts the reader** — no over-explaining. Senior audiences read between lines; the absence of explanation is itself a signal of seniority.
- **One dry aside per page maximum** — permission to be wry occasionally, but never a punchline. A pointed observation, a small subverted expectation.

### Reference voices

- Mike Monteiro's book voice (not his Twitter voice) — confident, sharp, restrained
- Erika Hall in *Just Enough Research* — declarative, slightly provocative, defensible
- *The New Yorker's* "Talk of the Town" — wry, knowing, never trying

## 4. Information architecture

Eight surfaces total. Each one earns its place by carrying weight. Visible navigation (no hamburger menu) with a clean footer carrying the full sitemap.

| ID | Surface | URL | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|
| 01 | Home | uxward.com | The 30-second answer. Editorial homepage; layer-cake, not feed. |
| 02 | Work | uxward.com/work | 5–7 selected case studies. Curated, not chronological. |
| 03 | Work / [case] | uxward.com/work/precocity | One template, applied with discipline. Hero is Precocity. |
| 04 | Writing | uxward.com/writing | Curated essays index with inline search/filter. Quarterly cadence. |
| 05 | Writing / [essay] | uxward.com/writing/[slug] | Long-form template. Owned platform; not Medium or Substack. |
| 06 | About | uxward.com/about | The receipts narrative. Portrait lives here. Selected talks section. |
| 07 | Contact | uxward.com/contact | Direct line. May be a red full-bleed page. Honest about availability. |
| 08 | Colophon | uxward.com/colophon | A small craft-signal page: typefaces, build, palette, philosophy. |

### What is deliberately absent

These were considered and rejected. They should NOT be added during design without explicit re-discussion:

- **No Services or Hire-Me page** — recruiters don't need it; consulting clients can find Contact
- **No Newsletter page or signup CTA** — this is not a content-creator site
- **No Manifesto or Design Philosophy page** — performative seriousness; the writing IS the manifesto, distributed across essays
- **No Speaking page** — selected talks appear in About as biographical evidence, not promotion
- **No Acting reel or multi-hyphenate sprawl** — Brandon is also an actor; that lives at brandonebward.com (a separate site), referenced once on uxward via a quiet About-page link
- **No Hamburger menu** — performative minimalism; eight surfaces fit visibly
- **No card grid for case studies** — typography carries the work; no thumbnails

## 5. The work — case study slate

### Hero artifact

**Precocity** — Brandon's 10-year build of design practice as a consultant at Precocity. This is the load-bearing piece on the entire site. Most measurable team and practice outcomes. The case study the homepage points hardest at.

This case study is the most important piece of writing on uxward. If shipping the rest of the site requires shipping it with rough drafts of the other cases, that is the right tradeoff. The Precocity case is non-negotiable.

### Full slate (5–7 cases, ordered editorially)

| # | Case | Type | Treatment |
|---|---|---|---|
| 01 | Precocity — the ten-year build | Practice / Leadership | **Hero** — visually distinct from others |
| 02 | Speakeazy | Founder / AI (solo SaaS, real users) | Standard |
| 03 | FORGE | Hands-on / AI (context-to-site builder) | Standard |
| 04 | Agency or senior consulting role | Practice / Strategy | Standard |
| 05 | Early-career startup build | Founder / Early-career | Standard |
| 06 (opt.) | Verity, RAD, or Figma plugin | Hands-on / Craft | Standard |

The slate read top-to-bottom narrates a 25-year arc: long-running build → recent founder moves → mid-career → early career. The curation is the design.

### Case study template

Every case study follows the same six-step structure (borrowed in spirit from Travis & Hodgson's UX research portfolio framework):

1. **Context** — what was the company / team / moment?
2. **The problem, named clearly** — what was broken at a business level?
3. **The approach** — what was done, why that way, and what was deliberately not done (senior judgment lives here)
4. **The work itself** — 2–3 selected artifacts with intent captions; restraint matters
5. **Outcomes** — what changed because of the work; numbers where they exist; *this section gets the red full-bleed pull*
6. **Reflection** — what would be done differently, what surprised, what was carried forward

## 6. Writing — content and structure

Brandon launches with approximately **28 essays already written**, organized into four thematic sections.

### The four sections

Using editorial "On..." framing (Sontag/Didion convention), in recommended order:

1. **On Leadership** — Hiring, scaling, holding the bar, telling the truth.
2. **On Experience Design** — The craft itself — IA, interaction, visual, systems.
3. **On Research** — How to know what you don't know.
4. **On AI** — Notes from working at the frontier.

This order is editorial: strategy → craft → rigor → frontier. It reads as a story: *this is who I am, this is how I work, this is what I value, this is where I'm pointed.*

### Cadence

**Quarterly** — four essays per year, going forward. This is the single most consequential constraint on the Writing page design.

A standard reverse-chronological feed makes quarterly cadence look like neglect ("latest: 4 months ago"). The page must look **composed in stasis** — deliberately curated, not staled-out. A visitor returning six months apart must see a page that reads as alive, not abandoned.

### Required Writing page features

- **Featured essay slot** at the top — single hero piece, manually curated (curator's pick, not "most recent")
- **Inline search/filter** — live filtering as the user types; filters by title and deck (not body, at launch); never navigates to a separate results page
- **Click-to-filter section headings** — clicking "On AI" collapses the other three sections; clicking again restores
- **No prominent dates above the fold** — no "Latest" labels, no pagination, no "X months ago" copy

## 7. Visual system

### Posture, in one sentence

**Editorial confidence, not graphic virtuosity.** The site wins on taste, not visual fireworks. Strong typographic hierarchy, generous whitespace, restraint executed with conviction.

Reference set: Khoi Vinh's *Subtraction.com*, Frank Chimero, Jason Santa Maria, Pentagram partner pages, *The New Yorker*.

NOT a reference set: Rally, Locomotive, Active Theory, or any agency-portfolio site with heavy motion, illustration, and visual flourish. Brandon explicitly does not position himself as a visual virtuoso — he positions on taste and strategic judgment. The visual system reflects this.

### Color tokens — locked

**Light mode (primary):**

| Token | Hex | Role |
|---|---|---|
| `--canvas` | `#FFFFFF` | Page background |
| `--ink` | `#040404` | Wordmark, primary headlines (not pure black — chosen) |
| `--body` | `#282828` | Body copy, secondary headlines |
| `--tertiary` | `#6B6B6B` | Captions, metadata, footer |
| `--rule` | `#E4E1DC` | Hairline rules, dividers |
| `--paper` | `#FAF8F4` | Tinted callout surface |
| `--signature` | `#CC0006` | Signature red — used as event, not ornament |

**Dark mode (designed to equal quality — not inverted):**

| Token | Hex | Role |
|---|---|---|
| `--canvas-dark` | `#121212` | Near-black canvas (not pure black) |
| `--ink-dark` | `#FFFFFF` | Pure white for primary headlines |
| `--body-dark` | `#E8E6E1` | Warm off-white for body (avoids cold-blue cast) |
| `--tertiary-dark` | `#9A958C` | Tertiary text |
| `--rule-dark` | `#2A2A2A` | Hairline rules |
| `--paper-dark` | `#1A1A1A` | Raised callout surface |
| `--signature-dark` | `#E5263A` | Red, retuned hotter for dark canvas |

### The red rule — non-negotiable

Red means **"this matters."** It is not decoration. It is not a section accent that recurs page-after-page. It is reserved for moments the page is asserting importance, and when it appears, it appears at full volume.

**Five sanctioned uses** across the entire site:

1. **Homepage** — the closing "Currently considering" full-bleed block (single most important recruiter signal on the site)
2. **Work index** — a red badge or marker on the hero case study only (Precocity); other cases stay neutral
3. **Case studies** — one red full-bleed band per case, carrying the most consequential outcome statement; white type on red
4. **Contact page** — may be a full-bleed red treatment, declarative and bold
5. **Earned editorial** — one specific essay per year, when warranted, may take a red treatment

Red is **never** used for body copy (contrast fails on white at small sizes), inline links inside paragraphs, recurring section accents, or hover states for navigation.

### Typography — locked

**Two families only. A third typeface is not permitted without explicit re-discussion.**

| Family | Source | Role |
|---|---|---|
| **Fraunces** (variable) | Google Fonts (Apache 2.0) | All editorial type — display, body, italic, captions. Four axes: opsz, wght, SOFT, WONK. |
| **JetBrains Mono** | Google Fonts (Apache 2.0) | Meta-text only — eyebrows, labels, captions, code. Quietly technical second voice. |

A formal type bake-off was conducted comparing Fraunces against Source Serif 4, Newsreader, and Cormorant Garamond. Fraunces won on personality, easter-egg potential, and the WONK axis (which is leveraged in the discovered-moments layer). Both faces are to be self-hosted in production (not loaded from Google Fonts CDN), subset to the glyphs actually used.

### Type scale

| Role | Family | Size | Weight | Line | Variation settings |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Display H1 | Fraunces | clamp(56px, 9vw, 124px) | 300 | 0.92 | opsz 144, SOFT 30, WONK 1 |
| Display H1 italic | Fraunces italic | same | 400 | same | opsz 144, SOFT 100, WONK 1 |
| Section H2 | Fraunces | clamp(36px, 5vw, 56px) | 300 | 1.05 | opsz 144, SOFT 30 |
| Subhead | Fraunces | 24–28px | 400 | 1.2 | opsz 96, SOFT 50 |
| Pull quote | Fraunces italic | 22–26px | 300 | 1.4 | opsz 96, SOFT 100, WONK 1 |
| Body | Fraunces | 17–18px | 400 | 1.65 | opsz 14 |
| Body emphasis | Fraunces | 17–18px | 500 | 1.65 | opsz 14 |
| Caption | Fraunces italic | 14–15px | 400 | 1.55 | opsz 14, SOFT 80, WONK 1 |
| Eyebrow / label | JetBrains Mono | 10–11px | 500 | 1.6 | letter-spacing 0.18–0.22em, uppercase |

### WONK axis usage rule

Fraunces' WONK axis (which makes italic letterforms more eccentric) is treated as a **discoverable feature, not a default**. Use it on:

- Italic display moments (H1 italics, H2 italics) at full WONK 1
- Italic captions and pull-quotes at full WONK 1
- Numerals at display size (case-study numbers) at WONK 1

Do NOT use WONK on body italic emphasis inside paragraphs (too mannered for the dry voice).

### Layout

- **Container:** 1180px max, 32px horizontal padding desktop, 20px mobile
- **Section structure:** Two-column grid with 100px left gutter for section number/marker, 1fr for body. The gutter is the editorial indent — gives the page a magazine-like rhythm. Collapses to single column on mobile.
- **Reading column max-width:** 580–720px for body prose
- **Vertical rhythm:** 96px between major sections; 48px between section header and body; 24px between body paragraphs

### Visual elements

- **Rules:** 1px solid `--rule` for hairlines; 2px solid `--ink` for major breaks; 3px solid `--signature` only for left borders on pull quotes
- **No rounded corners** — the site is editorial, not product-y; sharp corners or 2px maximum
- **No drop shadows** — depth comes from typography and whitespace, not from elevation effects
- **No illustration, no stock photography, no thumbnails** — typography carries the work; the only image on the site is the portrait on the About page

## 8. Discovered moments (easter eggs)

Five sanctioned signatures, same discipline as the red rule — rare, but unapologetic when used. No more than five.

| # | Egg | Where | Behavior |
|---|---|---|---|
| 01 | **Theatre mask** | Appears irregularly across the site — never on every page, never in same place twice | Click opens overlay with rotating Shakespeare quote on discovery/performance/depth + link to brandonebward.com (new tab) |
| 02 | **Wordmark direction shift** | The "ux" portion of `uxward` (rendered in signature red) | Every ~30s idle or on hover, hints at directional motion (subtle arrow, cursor block, faint shift) |
| 03 | **Color-mode toggle** | Header — replaces conventional sun/moon icon | Em-dash glyph that rotates 90° to vertical bar on click during mode transition |
| 04 | **404 page** | Anywhere broken | Large editorial type: *"This page doesn't exist. Most things don't."* One word in red. List of probable destinations. |
| 05 | **Reading-time bar** | Top of essay pages only | Thin red horizontal line, fills as the reader scrolls. No "X min read" copy — the bar IS the time signal. |

### Cut from consideration

These were considered and rejected. Do NOT reintroduce during design:

- Typewriter animations on the "currently considering" line (animation on critical recruitment content delays comprehension)
- Italic WONK on double-click of paragraph text (discoverability fails; nobody double-clicks paragraphs)
- Footer fleuron rotation on hover (animation for animation's sake)
- Case-study number gray-to-red flicker on hover (violates the red rule)

### Motion philosophy

Motion is restrained. **No section reveal animations, no scroll-jacking, no parallax, no "fade in as you scroll" patterns.** The page is composed in stasis and reads that way.

Sanctioned motion: the five easter eggs above, the mode-toggle transition (200ms cross-fade), hover color transitions on links (150ms, no transform/scale), and the reading-time bar (smooth scroll-tied fill). All motion respects `prefers-reduced-motion: reduce`.

## 9. Anti-patterns — explicitly prohibited

These were considered and rejected. They should not appear in any deliverable:

- **A manifesto or "design philosophy" page** — performative seriousness
- **A newsletter as primary CTA** — this is not a content-creator site
- **A hamburger menu** — performative minimalism
- **An animated hero, parallax, or video background** — type alone, opening
- **A "creative leader hub" framing or label** — the phrase is dead
- **Logos of past clients/employers** as a homepage element — reads as a brag wall
- **Stock photography or generic illustration**
- **Card grids with thumbnails for case studies** — typography carries the work
- **Pagination on the Writing index** — curated index, not a feed
- **Emoji, exclamation points, "hey there!" greetings** — voice violation
- **A separate Speaking page** — selected talks lives in About, not its own surface
- **An acting reel anywhere on uxward** — lives at brandonebward.com

## 10. Technical stack — locked

The stack is deliberately minimal. No CMS, no database, no content lock-in.

| Layer | Choice |
|---|---|
| Site generator | **Astro** — component-based, ships near-zero JS by default, opinionated content collections for case studies and essays |
| Content format | **Markdown files in a Git repo** — write in any editor (Cursor, iA Writer, Obsidian, VS Code), commit, the site rebuilds |
| Hosting | **Cloudflare Pages** — Git-push deployment, global CDN, free SSL, atomic deploys, preview URLs |
| Domain | **uxward.com**, registered on IONOS for email; DNS pointed at Cloudflare Pages |
| Versioning | **Git on GitHub** — the repo itself becomes part of the "still in the tools" evidence |
| Type loading | **Self-hosted Fraunces and JetBrains Mono**, subset to used glyphs (not Google Fonts CDN) |
| Analytics | **Cloudflare Web Analytics or Plausible** — privacy-respecting, no cookie banner |
| Writing search | **Client-side vanilla JS** for title/deck filter at launch; Pagefind later if body-text search is needed |

### Workflow

Write Markdown → commit → push → preview URL generated → merge to main → site rebuilds and deploys globally in under a minute. No CMS, no admin panel, no monthly hosting fees.

## 11. Accessibility — non-negotiable floor

- **Contrast:** body text on canvas ≥ 7:1 (WCAG AAA). Display text ≥ 4.5:1. Red on white at small sizes fails AA — which is why red is never used for body copy.
- **Focus states:** Visible, high-contrast outline on all interactive elements. Use 2px solid `--signature` as the focus ring.
- **Semantic HTML:** Heading hierarchy in order (h1 → h2 → h3). No skipped levels.
- **Touch targets:** 44×44px minimum on mobile.
- **Reduced motion:** All easter-egg animations and transitions respect `prefers-reduced-motion: reduce` with a static fallback.
- **Dark mode:** Designed to equal quality, not an afterthought.

| Phase | Scope |
|---|---|
| **0 — Foundations** | Tokens, type system, both color modes, homepage mock |
| **1 — Launch (v1.0)** | Homepage + Precocity case study + About + Contact + stub /work and /writing index pages with 1–2 pieces each. Real photo on About. |
| **2 — The slate (v1.1)** | Remaining 4–5 case studies, applied to the same template. Speakeazy and FORGE first (AI-tooling proof). |
| **3 — Writing (v1.2)** | All 28 launch essays published. Writing index page redesigned around the curated-not-feed posture with full search/filter functionality. |
| **4 — Polish (v1.3)** | Colophon page. Microinteractions. Performance tuning. The quiet refinements. |

**Cadence beats relaunch.** If any phase takes too long, ship lighter rather than delay. A v1.0 with three case studies and a strong Precocity is materially better than a v1.0 stuck in design for three months.

## 16. The one rule that governs everything

> **Fewer things, executed better.**

One signature color used as event. Two typefaces (no third permitted). Five sanctioned uses for red. Six homepage blocks. Five-to-seven case studies. Four 30-second answers. Five easter eggs. Four writing sections.

The discipline shows up everywhere because it IS the design. **Restraint is the senior move. The taste is in the cut.**

Anything proposed that breaks this principle should be challenged before being added.
